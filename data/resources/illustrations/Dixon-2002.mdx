## Abstract

This article explores the archaeological illustrations of Giovanni Battista Piranesi, a key figure in 18th-century classical archaeology and architectural studies. Susan Dixon investigates the sources of Piranesi’s illustrations and the impact they had on the understanding and interpretation of ancient Rome. The article also delves into how Piranesi's works were received, tracing their influence through subsequent centuries of architectural and archaeological studies.

## Purpose and Audience

This article is aimed at art historians, archaeologists, and scholars interested in classical studies, particularly those focused on 18th-century representations of ancient Rome. It also appeals to researchers of architectural history and visual culture, providing insights into the artistic techniques and historical significance of Piranesi's work.

## Key Excerpt

"Piranesi’s illustrations are not mere reproductions of ancient ruins but imaginative reconstructions that blend archaeology with a unique artistic vision. His ability to visualize the grandeur of Rome, both in its past and through his prints, left an indelible mark on the field of classical archaeology."
